Nacascolo

Bahía Culebra · Guanacaste · Greater Nicoya · Site 3047I-89-1

One of the most significant multi-component pre-Columbian sites in the Greater Nicoya cultural zone, Nacascolo (3047I-89-1) occupied a sheltered coastal valley on Bahía Culebra from at least 300 B.C. through the era of Spanish contact. Its elaborate mortuary record — polychrome ceramic assemblages, a Mesoamerican copper bell, red hematite pigment, combined primary and secondary burials — places it among the most archaeologically distinctive sites in southern Central America.

The Site and Its Setting

The Nacascolo site occupies a small valley of approximately one quarter square kilometer near the center of the peninsula that forms Bahía Culebra ("Snake Bay"), on the Pacific coast of Guanacaste. The valley is bounded on the north, south, and west by steep slopes of 80 to 100 meters, and on the east by the beach. Two principal seasonal streams traverse the valley from west to east, approximately 160 meters apart, and empty into an estuary — an estuarine setting the site shares with Puerto Culebra and Vidor, the other long-sequence sites in the bay, suggesting that brackish-water resources were central to subsistence strategies at all three locations.

Sixteen shell midden mounds were mapped across the site, from the vicinity of the caretaker's house to the northern and western slopes. A probable cemetery area was identified on the northern hillsides. The site's surface is scarred throughout by pot-hunters' holes — a century of intensive looting, concentrated especially in the area of Mound 1. Not a single sector escaped disturbance. The visible surface features are the shell middens alone, and their distribution defines the site's boundaries.

The ceramic record spans the full cultural sequence of Bahía Culebra: from the Mata de Uva and Culebra phases (A.D. 300–800) through the Panamá and Monte del Barco phases (A.D. 800–1200) — which account for nearly 62 percent of diagnostic surface sherds — into the Iguanita (A.D. 1200–1350) and Ruiz (A.D. 1350–1550) phases. The near-absence of pre-A.D. 500 material at the surface is not surprising: throughout the bay, deposits corresponding to the earlier Loma B and Orso phases are typically buried under several meters of alluvial soil.

The dominant ceramic phase is Monte del Barco (A.D. 1000–1200), which alone accounts for 35 percent of diagnostic sherds and whose assemblage includes Pataky Polychrome — a type apparently used exclusively in funerary contexts associated with high-status individuals, bearing painted motifs of strongly Mexican character (feathered serpent, sky dragon, dead head) that Wallace and Accola interpret as early local evidence of the population dislocations accompanying the Early Postclassic in the Mexican highlands.

Archaeological History

The first published account of Nacascolo was by J. F. Bransford (1881), who described the site briefly but accurately and noted the presence of mojones — columnar basalt rocks — in one of the shell middens, which local tradition identified as tomb markers. Earl Flint accompanied Bransford to the site and returned independently in March 1883, but never published his observations; his manuscript letters, now at the Peabody Museum, Harvard, survive.

In the late 1950s, Claude F. Baudez excavated the first proper stratigraphic test pit at Nacascolo, establishing the site within the regional cultural sequence. Doris Stone and Carlos Balser also excavated a small pit in one of the shell middens. A large portion of the site was destroyed by intensive looting between 1960 and 1970. In 1973, students from Beloit College made a reconnaissance and collected 70 ceramic sherds, now held by the Museo Nacional.

The first systematic excavations were carried out in summer 1978 by Henry Wallace (University of Arizona) and Richard M. Accola (University of Texas, Austin), under a broad survey and testing program in Bahía Culebra sponsored jointly by the Museo Nacional de Costa Rica and the Instituto Costarricense de Turismo. Their results, including the two complex Monte del Barco phase burials described below, were published in Vínculos, Vol. 6, Nos. 1–2 (1980), pp. 51–65 — the founding document of Nacascolo's scientific record, and the primary source for this catalogue.

NAC · 02 — DOCUMENTARY
Wallace & Accola (1980) — The Founding Excavation Report
Investigaciones Arqueológicas Preliminares de Nacascolo, Bahía Culebra

Published in Vínculos: Revista de Antropología del Museo Nacional de Costa Rica, Vol. 6, Nos. 1–2 (1980), pp. 51–65, by Henry Wallace (University of Arizona) and Richard M. Accola (University of Texas, Austin), this paper remains the primary published source for Nacascolo's archaeological record. It reports the results of four test excavations carried out in summer 1978 under a survey and testing program co-sponsored by the Museo Nacional de Costa Rica and the ICT.

The excavation technique was adapted to a heavily looted site: clean a pot-hunter's pit down to sterile deposits, record the stratigraphy, then excavate an adjacent undisturbed unit. Two sectors were tested: Mound 1 (a shell midden, Excavation E-2), and a flat zone to its west (Excavation E-1) where the looters had been less thorough. A test profile in the north wall of E-1 prompted continued excavation into apparently sterile black sand; at 1.71 meters depth the rim of a small vessel appeared, leading to the discovery of Burials 1 and 2 with their polychrome offerings still in place.

Burial 1 — Monte del Barco Phase (A.D. 1000–1200)

Primary interment: adult male, 30–40 years, supine and extended, oriented 12° northwest. Secondary remains of six additional individuals — two women, two men, one adult of indeterminate sex, one child aged 4–8 — placed over the thoracic cavity of the primary. Six left femurs were identified in the secondary assemblage, consistent with the age profiles of the six skulls.

Red hematite pigment had been scattered over the primary skeleton (thorax to femurs, concentrated at the pelvis) before placement of the secondary remains — the first published case of pigment scattering over a burial in Costa Rica.

Funerary offerings: 11 ceramic vessels and one annular base. Typologically: vessels 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10 — Pataky Polychrome; vessel 5 — Late Mora Polychrome; vessel 11 — Birmania Polychrome; vessel 12 — Santa Marta Polychrome (found nested inside vessel 1). Vessels 1 and 4 carry painted motifs of Mexican character: vessel 1, feathered serpent and late Mexica codex-style human figures; vessel 4, sky dragon and dead head motifs of apparently Toltec character.

Additional artifacts: shell bead bracelets on both wrists; on the left wrist, a copper bell — Pendergast Type IA3 (dated A.D. 1000–1200 from southern Mesoamerica), parallels at the Sacred Cenote at Chichén Itzá and at Zaculeu, Guatemala; a used polished stone celt of black stone; an irregularly hexagonal plate of iron pyrite or manganese oxide (possibly a mirror fragment); an artificially modified adult maxilla with saw marks, two perforations, and a polished lateral channel.

Burial 2 — Monte del Barco Phase (A.D. 1000–1200)

Located to the southeast of Burial 1, within the same cleaned pit. A multiple secondary interment: articulated skulls and mandibles of adult males, 20–35 years; a fragmentary red culinary vessel; and two blocks of dark gray hardened sand containing approximately 70% magnetite — probably a source of the black ceramic pigment common throughout the region.

Skull II bears filed teeth: Romero type A-1 grooves, most pronounced on the incisors but present also on the canines. At the time of publication, only two burials with dental modification had been reported in Costa Rica.

Paleopathology

Dental caries affected 34 teeth from the secondary skulls of Burial 1 and teeth from both skulls of Burial 2. Cranial osteoporosis was observed in all skulls. Three cases of osteomyelitis were found in the secondary long bones of Burial 1 (one chronic case in an adult tibia; two acute cases in adult fibulae), possibly representing part of the direct cause of death. Cribra orbitalia (porous type) was observed in one adult male skull. No traumatic pathology was present in either burial — a finding consistent with the Vidor skeletal population across the bay.

Notched Sherds

Nineteen deliberately notched ceramic sherds — flat pieces with two pairs of small notches on opposite sides, averaging 4.1 × 2.7 cm — were recovered from the burial fill. Parallels are known only from Greater Nicoya. Their function is debated: probably net weights, though Lange proposes they served as spacing gauges in net-weaving. The Nacascolo examples showed preserved white slip between the notch pairs, suggesting something was tied there.

Curatorial Notes

This catalogue holds both the original Spanish publication (Wallace & Accola 1980, Vínculos) and an English translation prepared for reference purposes. The original paper includes figures not reproduced here: a site plan (Fig. 1, 1979 survey), burial plan drawings (Figs. 2–3), artifact photographs (copper bell and shell beads, Fig. 4; modified maxilla and celt, Fig. 5; Pataky Polychrome vessels 3, 1, and 4, Fig. 6; filed mandible from Burial 2, Fig. 7; notched sherds, Fig. 8). These are candidates for future plates as photographic materials are sourced.

Playa Nacascolo today lies within the Four Seasons Resort at Peninsula Papagayo and is not accessible to the public. Material excavated in 1978 is held by the Museo Nacional de Costa Rica, San José. The complete scholarly bibliography for the site (Bransford 1881, 1884; Baudez 1959a, 1959b; Stone 1966, 1977; Accola 1978a, 1978c; Lange & Accola 1979) is detailed in the Wallace & Accola paper.

The NAC series is distinct from the EXC series (Alto del Cardal / Tierra Blanca, Cartago) and the FAR series (El Farallón Corobicí, Cañas). Nacascolo belongs to the Chorotega / Greater Nicoya cultural tradition; El Farallón is a Corobicí petroglyph site with Chibchan affiliations. These threads remain in separate catalogues.

Costa Rica Histórica · Chorotega / Greater Nicoya

Chorotega Place Names in Guanacaste

Landmarks, towns, and geographical features traceable to the pre-Columbian Chorotega culture

Name Description
Guanacaste Means "ear tree," named for the seeds of the Guanacaste tree, which resemble a human ear. Both the province and Costa Rica's national tree carry this Chorotega name.
Nicoya — the City The colonial city built directly on the ceremonial plaza of the Chorotega settlement of Chief Nicoa, the most powerful Chorotega ruler of the 16th century. Gil González Dávila's expedition arrived overland from the south in 1522–1523, having landed at a bay they named San Vicente — present-day Caldera — and marched north through the interior. When the Spaniards entered the town of Nicoya, Chief Nicoa gave González Dávila gold worth 14,000 castellanos and permitted himself and reportedly 6,000 subjects to be baptised. The expedition treasurer Cereceda counted 6,063 inhabitants under Nicoa's leadership — nearly five and a half times larger than the next biggest settlement encountered along the Pacific coast. The received account of a peaceful diplomatic encounter omits the ending: González reported that the inhabitants were converted to the Catholic faith before the cacique Nicoa was killed and large numbers of his people enslaved for export. The Church of San Blas, established 1522–1544 and rebuilt in calicanto masonry in 1644, stands on the site of the Chorotega ceremonial plaza described by the chronicler Oviedo in 1529. A corn plant — the sacred crop of the Chorotega — is carved into the church facade. In 2012, 154 burials dating from 1600 to 1813 were discovered beneath the church floor. An alternative etymology of the name from Nahuatl, Necoc Yáotl, means "with enemies on both sides" — a description that, in the end, proved accurate. Today Nicoya is the canton capital with a population of approximately 17,000.
Nicoya — the Peninsula The long geographical finger of land extending southward into the Pacific Ocean, forming the Gulf of Nicoya to the east. The heartland of Chorotega culture in Costa Rica, and home to the majority of the places documented in this catalogue: Guaitil, Nambi, Matambú, Nosara, Nandayure, Hojancha, Curime, Diriá, and the living Chorotega pottery tradition. The peninsula is also one of only five designated Blue Zones in the world — places identified by researcher Dan Buettner where people measurably live longer, healthier lives than the global average. The factors specific to the Nicoya Peninsula include exceptionally calcium- and magnesium-rich water derived from the local geology, the persistence of the traditional Chorotega diet of corn, beans, and squash in rural communities, continued physical activity into old age, and strong social networks. The beans that Nambi is named for, the corn carved on the facade of San Blas, the squash grown alongside them in the milpa for three thousand years — the Chorotega diet that sustained the builders of Nacascolo is now studied by epidemiologists as a model for human longevity.
Greater Nicoya The archaeological and cultural subarea defined by researchers, encompassing northwestern Costa Rica and Pacific Nicaragua — the territory within which the Chorotega and related cultures developed their ceramic traditions, trade networks, and political structures from approximately 500 B.C. to the Spanish conquest. Greater Nicoya is the framework within which Nacascolo, the polychrome ceramics (Papagayo, Pataky, and Granada polychromes), and the four chronological periods (Tempisque, Bagaces, Sapoá, and Ometepe) all make sense as part of a coherent cultural world. Recent excavations by the University of Calgary have substantially revised the chronology, demonstrating that sites and ceramics previously attributed to the later-arriving Nicarao were in fact Chorotega work, pushing the Chorotega cultural apogee earlier and longer than previously understood. Three distinct things — a city, a peninsula, an archaeological subarea — all bearing the name of one man, Chief Nicoa, who met the Spanish in 1523.
Gulf of Nicoya Costa Rica's largest gulf — a funnel-shaped tectonic estuary approximately 80 kilometres long and up to 50 kilometres wide, bordered by the Nicoya Peninsula to the west and fed at its head by the Río Tempisque. The gulf covers an area of 1,530 km², fringed by mangroves that serve as nurseries for marine species, nesting grounds for birds, and critical buffers against coastal erosion. It is the most important fishing ground in Costa Rica and the site of its main Pacific ports — the same waters from which the Chorotega drew fish as a dietary staple for over a thousand years. By the early 1990s the gulf was already identified as overexploited by shrimp trawlers and the longline fleet; subsequent research through 2015 indicates further deterioration, with most shrimp, eel, and catfish species declining significantly. The fourth dimension of a name that began with a Chorotega chief in 1523: a city, a peninsula, an archaeological subarea, and now the most ecologically pressured body of water in the country — all carrying the name of one man whose people fished these waters for centuries before the Spanish arrived.
Nandayure Named after a Chorotega princess. Her name combines nanda (stream) and yure (snake, flower, or bird), meaning "stream of the snake, flower, or bird." She was said to be the daughter of a powerful rival chief and a tragic figure in Chorotega legend. Her name lives on as a canton of Guanacaste.
Nosara Named after a Chorotega princess, daughter of Chief Nicoa. The name is thought to come from Chorotega roots, loosely translated as "you're not going anywhere." It survives today as both a river and a well-known beach and village.
Diriá Named after a famous Chorotega chief. Classified as a Chorotega toponym by linguists based on its geographic area, characteristic endings, and historical sources. It survives today as a district in Santa Cruz canton and as a river.
Curime Named after a Chorotega chief and high governor. The suffix -ime in Chorotega is said to indicate "abundance." Survives today as a barrio in Nicoya district.
Papagayo A former Chorotega settlement whose name persists today in the Gulf of Papagayo and the Papagayo Peninsula.
Pocosí Another former Chorotega settlement whose name has survived into the modern era as a place name in the region.
Playa Nacascolo A beach on the Papagayo Peninsula whose name derives from Nacaome, one of the pre-Columbian Chorotega towns recorded before the Spanish conquest. The site (designated G-89 Na) was an important and long-inhabited Chorotega settlement on Bahía Culebra, occupied from approximately A.D. 300 to 1550, and a significant pre-Columbian trading port between the cultures of the Intermediate Area and Mesoamerica. Archaeological excavations began in the 1960s and continued through the 1980s. The site was extensively looted by huaqueros (grave robbers) over more than a century before and during excavation; its polychrome ceramics were prized by collectors worldwide. The beach is now effectively enclosed within the Papagayo Peninsula Tourism Development Project — a deliberate initiative of the Instituto Costarricense de Turismo (ICT) from the 1990s that granted long-term concessions on public coastal land to international resort operators including the Four Seasons and Secrets resorts. While Costa Rican law technically guarantees public beach access, in practice Playa Nacascolo is accessible primarily to resort guests. The community with the most legitimate ancestral connection to the land — the Chorotega descendants at Matambú, a short drive away — had no seat at any of those tables.
Guaitil A Chorotega indigenous village named for the local dye-giving Guaitil tree, situated between Santa Cruz and Nicoya. It remains a living centre of pre-Columbian pottery tradition, where families hand-coil and fire vessels using techniques and natural pigments — terracotta, black, and white — passed down for over a thousand years. The authentic palette derives from local materials: the red and buff of the clay itself, black from ground stone, and white from a chalky mineral.
Nambi A locality on Route 21 just before Nicoya, home to a modest but rewarding workshop selling hand-painted Chorotega-style pottery alongside practical goods including hand-bound brooms. The word nambi carries remarkable range: it was the name of a powerful Chorotega chieftain of Nicoya; in Costa Rica it names a drought-resistant variety of beans — a staple crop of the Chorotega diet; and in the Río de la Plata region of South America it refers to a horse or mare with a drooping ear. A word that travelled, adapted, and survived across two continents.
Matambú The only officially recognised Chorotega indigenous reserve in Costa Rica, established in 1980 and located in the Hojancha and Nicoya cantons of Guanacaste Province. The etymology of the name itself is not clearly documented in the surviving linguistic record — one of many words whose meaning the extinction of the Chorotega language took with it. Matambú is the most complete human survival of the Chorotega world in the 21st century. Living ceremonial practice persists: the temescal, a traditional steam ceremony in which each stake driven into the ground is accompanied by prayer, leaves are burned, and the community begins by apologising to Mother Earth for any harm. A traditional farewell greeting also survives, in which fingers intertwine in a design resembling a snail shell — the spiral of infinity, friendship that never ends. The community's own name for their greeting is In Lak'Ech: I am the Other You. The reply is Hala Ken: You are the Other Me.
Playa Ocotal A beach just south of Playas del Coco on the Gulf of Papagayo. The name derives from the Nahuatl ocotl — pine resin or pine tree — combined with the Spanish suffix -al indicating abundance: "place of pines" or "place of pine resin." The ocote pine (Pinus oocarpa) was not merely a tree but technology — its resin provided the torches that lit pre-Columbian Mesoamerica at night. Ocotl appears in place names all the way from central Mexico down through the Chorotega migration corridor into Guanacaste, carried south with the people who depended on it.
Huacas A village in Carrillo canton, on the road between Filadelfia and Tamarindo. The name derives from the Quechua wak'a — a sacred object, place, or burial site, a spot where the spiritual world was understood to intersect with the physical. The word spread across Latin America during and after the colonial period as a general term for pre-Columbian sacred and burial sites, and was adopted by the huaqueros — grave robbers — as their professional descriptor. That a settlement here carries the name is consistent with the density of Chorotega occupation throughout Carrillo canton.
Tepayac A place name found in Guanacaste, derived from the Classical Nahuatl Tepeyacac — a compound of tepetl ("hill" or "mountain"), yacatl ("nose" or "point"), and the locative suffix -c ("at") — meaning "at the nose of the mountain" or "at the tip of the hill." The word is most celebrated as the name of the hill in what is now Mexico City where the Virgin of Guadalupe appeared in 1531 and where the Basilica of Our Lady of Guadalupe now stands. That Tepayac appears as a place name in Guanacaste reflects the Chorotega migration from central Mexico — they carried this Nahuatl topographic vocabulary southward, applying it to hills and ridges in the new landscape that resembled those they had left behind.
Guapinol A Nahuatl name meaning "flour tree," applied to the Hymenaea courbaril, one of the great trees of the Guanacaste dry forest. The name refers to the floury, edible pulp inside its dark reddish-brown seed pods. Several localities, rivers, and rural districts throughout Guanacaste carry the name, including hamlets in Santa Cruz and Nicoya cantons. The tree was central to pre-Columbian Mesoamerican life: its pod pulp was ground with corn, peanuts, and cacao to produce a ceremonial drink with nutritional, healing, and ritual properties; its resin — a form of copal — was burned as incense; and its exceptionally hard, durable timber was prized for construction.
Guacalito A place name found in Guanacaste and along the Gulf of Nicoya, derived from the Classical Nahuatl huacalli, meaning a wooden crate or vessel, combined with the Spanish diminutive -ito — thus "the little gourd" or "the small vessel." In Costa Rica and throughout Central America, a guacal is a calabash gourd (Crescentia cujete) used since ancient times as a drinking vessel, food container, and water carrier. A place named Guacalito suggests a spot where the calabash tree grew, or where the small vessels were made or used. The sacred cuauhxicalli — the eagle-gourd bowl of the Aztecs, used to hold offerings in ritual — shares the same Nahuatl root.
Cuajiniquil The name of a district in Santa Cruz canton on the Nicoya Peninsula, and also a settlement in La Cruz canton in the far north of Guanacaste. The word is of Nahuatl origin, derived from icxitlcuilli — icxitl meaning "foot" and cuil meaning "to bend" — giving the literal meaning "tree of the crooked foot" or "bent foot tree." It names the Inga flexuosa, a leguminous tree of the Fabaceae family known also as guaba de montaña, with long edible seed pods of thirty centimetres or more. The same naming logic as Guaitil: a place defined by the tree that grew there.
Curubandé A district of Liberia canton considered likely of Chorotega origin by linguists, though its precise etymological meaning remains uncertain.
Volcán Orosí A stratovolcano in the Cordillera de Guanacaste near the Nicaragua border, within the UNESCO World Heritage Guanacaste National Park. Orosí is a word of Chorotega origin — the name of an indigenous king of the region. Recent linguistic research suggests the Chorotega language was tri-vocalic and may not have included the letter o, raising the possibility that the original name was Urusí rather than Orosí. The first written record of the volcano under this name dates to 1745. A popular folk etymology holds that a priest climbing the volcano heard a mysterious voice reply "Plata no, oro sí" — "Silver no, gold yes" — but the Chorotega king and the linguistic evidence predate the priest by centuries. The volcano last erupted approximately 3,500 years ago.
Río Tempisque The major river running through Guanacaste, the hydrological axis of the province. The name is considered to share its Nahuatl/Chorotega linguistic roots alongside place names such as Guanacaste and Nicoya.

The Chorotega spoke Mangue, a member of the Oto-Manguean language family. Many place names blend Chorotega cultural identity with Nahuatl linguistic roots. While some names — like Nicoya — are documented in Spanish colonial records, others are partly rooted in oral tradition. The language itself was extinct by the 18th century in Costa Rica. · Primary sources: Quirós Rodríguez (2002) for Chorotega linguistics; Accola (1980) for Nacascolo; McCafferty & Steinbrenner (2022) for Greater Nicoya archaeology.
